📖 Beginner's Guide

How to Build Your
First Disc Golf Bag

Most beginners buy the wrong discs. They grab whatever looks cool, end up with a bag full of high-speed drivers they can't throw, and wonder why the game feels impossible. This guide will save you that money and frustration.

Understanding Flight Numbers

Every disc golf disc has four numbers printed on it. These are called flight numbers, and they describe how the disc flies. Understanding them is the single most important skill for building a bag that actually works for you.

Speed
1 – 14
How fast you need to throw it. Higher = more arm speed required. Beginners should stay under speed 7.
Glide
1 – 7
How long it stays in the air. Higher glide = more distance potential. Great for beginners.
Turn
-5 – +1
High-speed tendency. Negative = turns right (RHBH). Beginners want -1 to -3 for straighter flights.
Fade
0 – 5
Low-speed finish. Higher = harder left hook at the end. Beginners want 0-2 for predictable finishes.
⚠️ The Biggest Beginner Mistake

Buying a distance driver (speed 10–14) as your first disc. At low arm speeds, high-speed drivers don't spin fast enough to be stable — they fly hard right (for RHBH throwers) and go nowhere. Start with discs rated speed 5–7.

The Four Types of Discs

Putters (Speed 1–3)

The most important discs in your bag. Slow, predictable, and forgiving. Great for approach shots inside 150ft and for learning the throwing motion. Beginners often make the mistake of ignoring putters — don't.

Good starter options: Innova Aviar, Discraft Jawbreaker Luna, Dynamic Discs Judge

Midranges (Speed 4–6)

The backbone of a beginner's bag. These fly straight, go far enough for most shots, and are very forgiving of technique mistakes. A beginner with a solid midrange can play most courses effectively.

Good starter options: Innova Mako3, Discraft Buzzz, Dynamic Discs Verdict

Fairway Drivers (Speed 7–8)

The first driver type beginners should try. Lower speed requirements than distance drivers, still give you more distance than a midrange when thrown correctly. Add these once you can consistently throw a midrange 200ft+.

Good starter options: Innova Leopard3, Discraft Undertaker, Latitude 64 River

Distance Drivers (Speed 9–14)

Only useful when you can generate enough arm speed to make them work — typically 280ft+ consistent distance. Until then, they turn over uncontrollably and go shorter than your midrange.

Understanding Disc Stability

Stability describes a disc's tendency to curve during flight. For a right-handed backhand (RHBH) thrower:

Overstable discs finish left. They fight headwinds well and are reliable in windy conditions. For beginners, overstable discs can go very far right before a hard left finish — hard to control early on.

Stable / Neutral discs fly relatively straight. These are your workhorses — they do exactly what you expect and are beginner-friendly.

Understable discs turn right during flight (RHBH). These are beginner-friendly at low arm speeds because they naturally compensate for the low spin you generate — they still fly relatively straight instead of fading hard left.

💡 Stability Formula

Add Turn + Fade together. Positive result = overstable. Near zero = neutral. Negative result = understable. A disc with turn -3 and fade 1 has a stability of -2 — nicely understable for beginners.

Building Your First Bag

You don't need 20 discs to play well. Here are three bag builds based on how many discs you want to start with:

🌱 Starter Bag — 3 Discs
  • 1× Putter — for putting and short approach shots (e.g. Aviar, Judge)
  • 1× Understable Midrange — your go-to disc for most shots (e.g. Mako3, Comet)
  • 1× Understable Fairway Driver — for longer holes (e.g. Leopard3, River)
⚡ Intermediate Bag — 7 Discs
  • 1× Dedicated Putter — for inside the circle putting
  • 1× Approach Putter — slightly more overstable for bump-and-run shots
  • 1× Understable Midrange — straight shots and rollers
  • 1× Neutral Midrange — workhorse for 150–220ft
  • 1× Understable Fairway Driver — turnover shots and max distance
  • 1× Neutral Fairway Driver — straight lines, slight fade
  • 1× Overstable Fairway Driver — headwinds and forced fades

Matching Discs to Your Arm Speed

Arm speed is the most important factor in disc selection — more important than brand, plastic, or what your favourite player throws. Here's a rough guide:

Under 200ft: Stick to putters and understable midranges only. Speed 1–5.

200–280ft: Add fairway drivers. Speed 5–8. Distance drivers will turn over.

280–350ft: You can begin to use entry-level distance drivers. Speed 7–10.

350ft+: Most drivers in your bag will fly as intended. Speed 9–13 available.

Does Plastic Type Matter?

Yes — but not in the way most beginners think. Plastic affects durability, grip, and how quickly a disc wears, not the fundamental flight characteristics.

Premium plastics (Star, Champion, Z-Line, Lucid) are slippery when new, wear slowly, and hold their original flight shape for a long time. Good for discs you want to stay predictable.

Base plastics (DX, Pro-D, Base Grip) are grippy, cheap, and beat in quickly. A beat-in base plastic disc becomes understable and is great for beginners because it flies straighter at low arm speeds.

✅ Beginner Plastic Tip

Start with base plastic putters and midranges. They're cheap, grippy, and naturally become more understable as they wear — which actually helps beginners throw straighter. Save the premium plastic for when you know exactly which discs you love.
