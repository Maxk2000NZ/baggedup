Disc Flight Numbers Explained

Speed, glide, turn, and fade — what they mean, how to read them, and how to use them to pick the right disc for any shot.

Every disc has four numbers printed on it. These numbers — called flight ratings — describe exactly how that disc will behave in the air when thrown at the appropriate speed. Understanding them changes everything.

Speed (1–14)

Speed is how fast the disc needs to travel to fly as rated. A speed 13 driver needs serious arm speed to work properly. Beginners should stay at speed 7 or below — anything faster just goes right into the ground if you can't generate enough spin and velocity.

Glide (1–7)

Glide describes how long the disc stays airborne. High glide (6–7) equals more distance in calm conditions. Low glide means the disc drops quickly — useful for windy days or short shots where you don't want the disc to float.

Turn (-5 to +1)

Turn is what the disc does at the start of its flight. Negative numbers mean the disc turns right (for right-hand backhand). A -3 disc will arc right before fading back. Beginners love high-turn discs because they're easier to throw far.

Fade (0–5)

Fade is the hard left hook at the end of the flight. A 5 fade disc will make a strong, reliable finish left — great for getting around obstacles. A 0 fade disc lands almost straight.

🎯 Quick rule: Low speed + high turn = beginner-friendly. High speed + high fade = advanced overstable.
  • A 9/5/-2/1 fairway driver: fast, floaty, slight right turn, minimal fade
  • A 7/5/0/1 midrange: moderate speed, very controllable, predictable
  • A 3/4/0/2 putter: slow, grippy, reliable short-range approach disc

Why Arm Speed Matters More Than Your Discs

The biggest mistake in disc golf is buying discs rated for arm speeds you don't have. Here's how to match your bag to your actual ability.

Walk into any disc golf shop and you'll see walls of drivers rated speed 10, 11, 12, 13. These are marketed as distance machines. And they are — but only if you can throw them fast enough. Most recreational players can't.

What arm speed actually means

Arm speed is the velocity you generate through your reach-back and pull-through. It directly determines whether a high-speed disc will fly as rated or simply roll over and crash. A speed 12 disc needs somewhere around 65-70mph at release to fly properly. Most beginners throw closer to 40-45mph.

The damage a wrong disc does

When you throw an overstable high-speed driver too slowly, it doesn't just go shorter. It actively turns and crashes to the ground early, often going less distance than your midrange. Worse, it teaches your body wrong release habits as you unconsciously compensate.

📏 Rule of thumb: If you're throwing under 250 feet, keep your drivers below speed 9. If under 200 feet, stick to midranges and putters.

How to find your actual speed range

  • Throw a very understable disc (like an Innova Leopard). If it goes left on a RHBH throw, you're throwing hard enough for that speed
  • Work up in speed only when your current max driver is turning over reliably

Overstable vs Understable — When to Use Each

Understanding disc stability is what separates recreational players from competitive ones. Learn when to reach for each type and why.

Stability describes a disc's tendency to fade left (overstable) or turn right (understable) during flight, for a right-handed backhand thrower. Every disc sits somewhere on this spectrum, and knowing when to use each is a genuine skill.

Overstable discs

Overstable discs want to fade hard left. They're reliable in the wind (headwinds won't flip them over), great for hyzer-line shots, and useful for getting around obstacles on the left side of a fairway. They're predictable — where you release them is roughly where they'll end up, just left.

Understable discs

Understable discs turn right before fading. This makes them ideal for long distance throws (the turn adds rollout), roller shots, and right-side lines that would be hard to hit with an overstable disc. They're also much easier for beginners to throw far because they don't fight the slower arm speed.

Neutral discs

The sweet spot — a disc that goes mostly straight with a gentle fade. These are the workhorses of a good bag. Your go-to midrange and main fairway driver should probably be neutral.

💡 In a headwind, reach for overstable. In a tailwind, go understable. In calm conditions, neutral is your friend.
  • Overstable examples: Discraft Buzzz OS, Innova Firebird, Dynamic Discs Felon
  • Understable examples: Innova Leopard, Latitude 64 River, Westside Harp (for putters)
  • Neutral examples: Discraft Buzzz, Innova Roc3, Dynamic Discs Justice

Disc Selection in Wind: A Complete Playbook

Headwind, tailwind, crosswind — each requires a completely different approach. Learn how wind changes your disc selection.

Wind is the great equaliser in disc golf. Even pros regularly make the wrong disc choice in tricky conditions. Here's a systematic way to think about it.

Headwinds (wind in your face)

Headwinds amplify the disc's high-speed characteristics. An understable disc that would normally turn just a bit in calm conditions will flip completely over in a headwind. The solution: throw more overstable than you think you need. Hyzer angles also help fight the headwind. Reduce power slightly — a smoother release beats a muscled throw in a headwind every time.

Tailwinds (wind behind you)

Tailwinds are deceptive. They feel like they should help, and they can — but they also make overstable discs fade harder and faster. Your go-to driver might finish hard left before it should. Counter this with slightly understable discs and flat or anhyzer release angles.

Crosswinds

Wind from the left pushes the disc right — you want something with extra fade to counteract drift. Wind from the right is trickier; use an understable disc and let the wind help rather than fight it.

🌬 Use BaggedUp's wind simulation to see exactly how your current bag responds — before you're standing on the tee wondering.

Quick reference

  • Headwind: more overstable, hyzer release, less power
  • Tailwind: more understable, flat or anny release, let it fly
  • Left crosswind: more overstable to counteract drift
  • Right crosswind: understable and let the wind work for you

How to Spot Gaps in Your Disc Golf Bag

Most players have overlapping discs doing the same job while missing entire shot shapes. Learn how to audit your bag and fix it.

A bag gap isn't just about missing discs — it's about missing shot shapes. You might have 14 discs and still be completely defenceless on a hyzer flip line or a 200-foot forced right-to-left shot.

The 4 shot shapes every bag needs

  • Straight with fade — your reliable workhorse for straight fairways
  • Hyzer line — for shots that need to go left and stay left
  • Anhyzer / flip — for long right-curving shots or big turnover lines
  • Finesse / approach — a slow, accurate disc for 80-150 feet

The distance ladder

A well-built bag covers a range of distances in roughly equal steps. If you jump from a 50-foot putter to a 300-foot driver with nothing in between, you'll always be throwing either too hard or too easy on approach shots.

Using BaggedUp's heatmap

The mold slot heatmap shows a 14×3 grid of all possible speed/stability combinations. Dark squares mean you have coverage there. Empty squares are gaps. A good bag should fill the grid without too many duplicates in the same zone.

🔥 The most common gap: intermediate players almost always lack a dedicated 100-180 foot overstable approach disc. A beat-up midrange or dedicated approach disc fixes most short-game struggles overnight.

Disc Golf Plastic Guide: Which Type Should You Buy?

Base, premium, metal flake, glow — plastic affects grip, durability, and how the disc flies as it wears. A plain-language guide for every budget.

Disc plastic is one of the most confusing parts of buying discs because every manufacturer uses different names for the same basic types. Innova calls their premium plastic "Star." Discraft calls theirs "Z." Discmania calls it "S-Line." They're all doing roughly the same thing.

Base plastic (DX, Pro-D, D-Line)

The most affordable option. Grippy in cold weather, flexible, and wears in quickly — which actually makes overstable discs more understable over time. Great for beginners who want to try lots of molds cheaply. Not ideal for tournament play.

Premium plastic (Star, Z, S-Line, Gold)

More durable, holds its flight characteristics longer, better grip in all conditions. The standard for serious players. If you find a disc you love, buy it in premium so it stays consistent.

Metal flake / sparkle plastics

Premium plastic with added metallic flakes for aesthetics. Fly identically to standard premium. Usually slightly pricier but worth it if you want to show off a bit.

Special blends (Halo, Plasma, Neo)

These are manufacturer-specific premium blends that often feel slightly stiffer or more grippy than standard premium. Largely preference-based — try a few and decide what you like in your hand.

💡 For your first disc: buy base plastic to learn the mold cheaply. Once you love it, upgrade to premium for consistency. See our full plastic comparison chart at baggedup.vercel.app/plastics.html

Tournament Bag vs Practice Bag — Should You Run Two?

Many competitive players maintain separate bags for different scenarios. Here's when it makes sense, and how to manage it.

Running two bags isn't just for pros. Any player who plays both competitive rounds and casual practice can benefit from a dedicated setup for each — because the disc selection priorities are genuinely different.

The tournament bag

Optimised for reliability and shot coverage. Every disc should have a defined role. You want your most consistent, premium-plastic versions of your favourite molds. Nothing experimental. Nothing you haven't thrown hundreds of times. Typically 9–12 discs.

The practice bag

Where you experiment. Beat-in base plastics to learn how different stabilities feel. New molds you're testing. Shots you wouldn't risk in a tournament round. This bag can be a mess — it's supposed to be.

Hyzer vs Anhyzer: Mastering Release Angles

Your release angle is one of the most powerful tools in disc golf — and most beginners don't know they have control over it.

When you throw a disc, the angle of your wrist and forearm at release determines what the disc does first. This isn't about power or technique — it's a deliberate choice that unlocks a massive range of shot shapes.

Flat release

The disc is perfectly level at release. It will fly according to its natural flight numbers. This is your baseline throw — learn this before anything else.

Hyzer

The outside edge of the disc is tilted down at release. The disc naturally wants to stay on that angle and fade harder left. Hyzer releases are reliable and predictable — the disc is fighting to go one way and you're helping it. Great for shots that need to go left and stay there.

Anhyzer (anny)

The outside edge is tilted up at release. The disc goes right first before fading back. An anny release with an understable disc can go very far right. An anny release with a neutral disc gives you a long sweeping right curve that comes back left at the end. This shot is called a "hyzer flip" when executed perfectly.

🎯 The hyzer flip: throw a mildly understable disc on a steep anhyzer angle. The disc "flips" up to flat and then glides straight with maximum distance. It's the shot pros use for big drives in open conditions.
  • Hyzer + overstable = hard left, very reliable in wind
  • Flat + neutral = straight with gentle fade
  • Anhyzer + understable = big right-to-left sweeping shot
  • Anhyzer + overstable = straight shot (the two cancel out)

Course Management: Score Lower Without Throwing Further

The biggest scoring improvements rarely come from more distance. They come from smarter decisions with the discs you already have.

Watch a scratch player and a 10+ handicap player throw together and you'll often notice they throw similar distances. The difference is almost entirely decision-making. The scratch player rarely goes for the hero shot. They play to their strengths, avoid trouble, and make putts.

Play to the fat part of the fairway

Every hole has a "safe zone" — the widest, clearest path that avoids OB, trees, and water. Aim for that zone even if it's not the most direct line. A bogey from the safe zone beats a double from the hero line.

Know your miss

Every disc you throw has a characteristic miss. Your main driver might reliably miss slightly right on a tight release. Use that knowledge — aim left of your target so your miss still ends up in a good spot.

Putt to make, not to impress

From outside circle 1 (10 metres), the goal is a "stress-free" putt — hit the chains or land in the basket. Don't always throw for the hero ace from 25 metres when a controlled shot to 4 metres gives you a tap-in bogey.

🧠 BaggedUp tip: use the AI coach and describe a specific hole you're struggling with. Give it the layout, your bag, and the problem. It will suggest both disc selection and a strategic game plan.
  • Eliminate the double bogey — it destroys scorecards
  • Know the hole before the round — walk it if you can
  • Pick a specific landing zone, not just "down there"
  • Count your birdies on holes where you have distance advantage

Forehand vs Backhand: When to Use Each

Most disc golfers have a dominant throw — but the best players use both hands of the clock. Here's when to choose forehand.

A strong forehand (sidearm) is arguably the biggest competitive advantage you can develop after your first year of disc golf. It opens up shot shapes that are impossible or very difficult with a pure backhand game.

When forehand wins

  • Right-side obstacles that a backhand would have to hyzer around
  • Tight right-to-left dogleg holes where a forehand natural fade lines up perfectly
  • Low ceiling shots — forehands can stay low without fighting hyzer physics
  • Quick approach shots where winding up a full backhand isn't practical

Disc selection for forehands

Forehands put more torque on the disc than backhands. Understable discs will flip badly with a forehand. You want overstable or at least neutral discs for forehand throws — the same "overstable" disc that's reliable on a forehand might be too overstable to use backhand.

Building your forehand

Start with a midrange, not a driver. A speed 5-7 disc with a slight overstable bias (0/1 to 0/3) will teach your forehand release without the disc rolling over. The Discraft Comet and Innova Mako3 are popular learning tools.
